RSA is a popular algorithm for public key cryptography that is widely used for secure communication and authentication on the internet. It was invented in 1977 by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman, and it is named after the initials of their last names.

RSA is based on the concept of a "key pair," consisting of a public key and a private key. The public key is a code that is widely available and can be used by anyone to encrypt a message. The private key is a code that is kept secret and is used by the owner of the key pair to decrypt messages that were encrypted with the corresponding public key.

One of the key benefits of RSA is that it allows for secure communication without the need for a shared secret key. This means that two parties can communicate securely even if they have never met before and do not have a shared secret. RSA also enables the creation of digital signatures to verify the authenticity and integrity of messages.
